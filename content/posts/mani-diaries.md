Hello. Before leaving you alone with my Mani chronicles, which are full of literary references and a bit of theatricality, there is an important point I must mention: this piece is a semi-travelogue, semi-indistinguishable tale, translated directly back into English from the diary I tried to keep in Italian during my time in Greece. However, since my Italian is not one of my shining qualities, I often struggled to understand what I meant to say and tried to capture the meaning without damaging the writing. As in every good story, there is a bit of that within these as well. Now, the dear reader might ask: "O author, why are you turning these events you’ve just lived through from a diary into a travel summary?" Since I couldn't think of a cleverly arrogant answer fast enough for them, those readers will certainly be answered in my future posts.



## The Mani Chronicles
Call me Alp. I have always felt a certain kinship with Melville’s Ishmael; I am such a person: when my taste is soured, when a damp, drizzly November enters my soul, when I find myself following every funeral procession without notice, or when the gloom within me grows so heavy that—if some moral principles did not stop me—I would go out to the street and deliberately take people's hats off their heads and throw them to the ground.

When I reached this point, with my money low and nothing particularly tying me to my home, besides, whenever nothing binds me to a place, I begin to lose even the joy of looking into people's eyes, and I start to inwardly envy yet despise the physical and worldly troubles of mankind. I arrogantly tell myself how they squander their existence on such wretched earthly cares instead of noble, timeless woes like mine. It is then I realize I must throw myself into the midst of physical hardships. And to be honest, dear reader, when those hardships actually find me, my existential angst vanishes without a trace, and I then grow angry at those ungrateful, pampered children for being so oblivious to real suffering. I try to understand which is more unbearable in this pendulum that swings from one restlessness to another. This is my substitute for pistol and ball; Cato may throw himself upon his sword with a philosophical flourish, but I quietly got on a plane.

![Dilemma of Cow](/images/cow.jpeg)

For this goal, the village of Pyrgos Dirou in the Mani region of Greece seemed like an intriguing option; I wanted to relive the longing for one of my hometowns, Datça—now ravaged by modern conditions—and, to be honest, to experience the Eastern Mediterranean cuisine once again. It is worth mentioning a bit about this village and Mani, as I was to spend two months here. It was a landscape of stone fortresses, a closed-off geography of mountains and seas very close to the settlements, where the arrival of spring was heralded by a rainbow pattern over a green undertone. Being on ancient Spartan lands and claimed as the starting point of the Greek Revolution, the people were stern and proud of their warrior history. This place should have been the ideal environment for me, someone who wanted to live as directly and as generally as a Spartan.

When I arrived, I was assigned a spacious but old Greek village house, not much different from the region of my own origins; just like the Aegean Turks, the culture of the Aegean Greeks shows great similarities, save for religion. From the deer-patterned rugs on the walls to the local traditional clothing, the resemblance was striking. As for my employers—let’s call them Mr. and Mrs. Doe—they were unique individuals. Mrs. Doe was my link to this world; my tasks and all my communication with other Greeks were entrusted to her. She was hardworking, organized, and extremely attentive, yet unlike typical Mediterranean mothers, she wasn't particularly maternal. Her husband, Mr. Doe, was a Maniot through and through. Since he was always engaged in physical labor around the village, he was a highly skilled and direct person. Because he constantly spoke in a high-pitched, booming voice, for a while I thought he was perpetually angry—a man hardened by the harsh conditions of life.

So, what was my job to be? My work generally consisted of the overall care of horses—which involved moving them from one place to another, feeding, washing, mucking out, and giving riding lessons—alongside tending to the tasks of the farm, as it was also an olive farm. However, on this farm with over twenty horses and other livestock, the stress of the labor performed an "equivalent exchange," much like the first principle of alchemy, with the tranquility of the landscape.

Mrs. Doe, noticing my distracted nature, would constantly scold me for my lack of focus and take my book away from me, claiming it diverted my attention; she wanted me to just watch the movement of the horses in my spare moments. But employers who have left their youth behind, let me scold you a bit: beware of those pale-faced, hollow-eyed young men who fall into untimely thoughts—those whose heads are filled not with the Protestant Ethic, but with Phaedo. No good comes from them; they will circumambulate your farm a thousand times like a devotee of Plato, but they won't earn you a single cent.

I must tell you this, because these days, many troubled, distracted, and romantic young men, disgusted by worldly anxieties, seek refuge on farms, longing to be moved amidst the scent of manure and olive oil. Farm owners frequently scold these pensive young philosophers, throwing in their faces how they take no interest in their duties; they tell them, more or less openly, that they have no intention of earning an honest living and that, deep down, they almost wish not to see a single horse. But whatever they say is in vain! For these young Platonists have convinced themselves that their eyes do not see well—that they are shortsighted.

Mr. Doe, on the other hand, represents the physical side of the labor; he constantly grumbles about the horses, gets highly frustrated with my inefficient moves, and putting his hands to his head, he shouts, "Think!" He criticizes my naive nature toward the horses. If you ask how I understood this without knowing Greek, I would tell you it's because his attitude toward the horses is identical to his attitude toward me. But make no mistake, this doesn't mean the man doesn't love the horses; it is simply that the discord among humans regarding how they show their love is a story as old as time itself.

Once, when he saw me drop the reins while leading two horses, he lost his temper and began a magnificent scolding in Greek; while I was expected to understand the problem solely from those Greek reprimands, my mind, wandering around with Wittgenstein, wondered if I could have understood a lion better had it been able to speak. I didn't mind much when Mr. Doe scolded me in the heat of work. What of it? Is it such a heavy insult if an old farmer tells me to muck out horse manure? How much does it weigh in the scales of the New Testament? Do I fall from Gabriel’s grace just because I respected the man and did exactly as he said? Who ain't a slave in this world, I ask you? In short, let old farmers bluster and shove me as much as they like; I see no harm in it. I know that this happens to everyone—whether from a physical or a metaphysical standpoint. This is the order of the world. All humans are thus pushed and shoved. Everyone should pat each other on the back and endure their destiny.

Besides his scolding, he was directly involved in my own riding lessons. He would get very angry when I did the trotting incorrectly or failed to stop the horse immediately on my command, constantly urging me on with shouts of "pame, pame!" (πάμε, πάμε!). We would ride through the village together on the horses named Keravnos and Dukati. Since we had no common language, he would use the only words he had taught me—ti kanis (τι κάνεις) and kala (καλά)—to ask, "Ti kanis, Alp?" (Τι κάνεις, Αλπ;). And when I replied "kala" (καλά) and he said "oreos" (ωραίος), he would simply continue on his way. Even now, I still do not know the opposite of kala, and I wonder inwardly if this is for the better. Should a person, perhaps, only be able to say "I am well"?

During my free time at work, I would sit at my desk with its beautiful view, assigned to me on the farm, and begin to listen to everything around the farm alongside the sounds of the birds. Since taking anything else into my hands during these breaks was not welcomed, I was left alone with my thoughts in abundance. In those moments, Nadja, a Dutch lady and a regular customer of the farm, would accompany me, and we would talk extensively about books and horses. She must have felt pity for me since I couldn't fully talk to anyone; she was kindhearted enough to invite me to a pizza night with her family at her house, which was home to five dogs.

Due to the Mani Independence celebrations, my workload at the farm had increased. Just as I was counting the days to return to my urban life, weary from the rigors of the work pace, Mr. Doe gave me a cauldron on a particularly busy day and told me to build a large fire. He strictly warned me not to let the fire go out and told me to wait for Mr. Nikos. I must mention Mr. Nikos a bit: he is a man I believe to be in his early 70s, very skillful but slow-moving. I had the chance to work with him a few times; perhaps because he didn't want to tire me, he would continue working on his own to such an extent that it made me question my own existence, never asking for help unless he absolutely needed it. His breaks were quite long. Knowing this man’s slow nature, I was aware that I would have to keep this fire alive for a long time.

![Firelink Shrine](/images/firelink.gif)

And this task was quite an enjoyable one; as someone who had dreamed of sitting by the fire in Firelink Shrine or Majula in Dark Souls, my own "fire-keeping" was unique. Watching the horizon where the sea merged with the matchless greenery, looking at the Orthodox church that justified the sunset by partially obscuring its stone grandeur, and reciting countless lines about fire from Game of Thrones to myself while lost in thought—that three-hour wait didn't feel like three hours at all. I believe that most people, most of the time, truly long for moments like these without even realizing it; however, such a moment must spring from necessity, not as a mere product of romanticism. This vigil and these thoughts came to an end with the arrival of Mr. Nikos, and Mr. Nikos had not come empty-handed; he arrived with two dead pigs in the back of his truck.

After Mr. Nikos and I carried the two dead pigs to two different tables, he thrust a knife into my hand and wanted me to do exactly as he did. This request consisted of using the knife like a razor to shave the hair and skin the pig; it was a joyless and long process. The body of a creature that had only recently died was entrusted to me, and like a scavenger, I was trying to plunder a newly vacated house. As I grew weary of the difficulty of the task, I realized that if I were to stop and leave now, other elements of nature would simply seize this body; it was, in every sense, a race. After all, my own body will also vanish through the race of worms and soil bacteria. Yet, I couldn't help but feel a pang of sadness while looking into the face of the dead pig. However, this same pig was to become the source of joy for the dinner of people who had come from all over Greece—and indeed the world—for the grand barbecue the following day.

When I went to work the next day, I was expecting a heavy workload for the evening barbecue, but Mr. Doe kept telling me, "Sit, relax." Fearing this might be a sarcastic warning, I kept trying to find something to do. Finally, he lost his temper and told me to go home and come back for dinner in the evening. When I returned that night, Mrs. Doe—apparently fed up with me wandering around the kitchen like a chicken with its head cut off—said she didn’t need any help and sat me at a table in the middle of the crowd, luckily next to another Dutch couple.

For some reason, my closest friends during this process were always Dutch. As for this Dutch couple—let’s call them the Van Pelts—Mrs. Van Pelt was a very naive, attentive listener with a delicate nature, and it was obvious she was quite intelligent. Her husband was more jovial and social. While Mrs. Van Pelt was asking small talk questions out of politeness, she asked me where in Turkey I was from. Since most non-Turks wouldn't know Datça, I gave my usual dismissive answer: "Izmir." As our conversation progressed, I learned that before moving to Greece, they had also considered Datça in Turkey. When I then said, "Actually, I am from Datça," she asked, "Why didn't you say so before?" and I replied, "I didn't think you would know it." We both burst into laughter, and this completely melted the ice.

During the process, our conversation turned toward horses. Here, we must mention a bit about what horses mean to me and the difference in methods at the farm where I worked. By nature, and due to the way they are raised by humans, horses are animals that are always fearful and cannot tolerate stress. Most horse farms, especially those with old-fashioned methods, try to hand the reins to these two-legged creatures by, in a way, exploiting the horses' stress. However, perhaps because I am unnecessarily naive, I do not easily tug at a horse's rope or try to intimidate them. Mr. Doe scolds me plenty for this, as it goes against the rigid discipline they try to instill in the horses. Yet, Mrs. Van Pelt said she found it more accurate to describe the horses on her own farm as "curious" rather than "tense." She explained that the incredibly curious and skeptical nature of horses would reach its goal if given enough time and patience; and frankly, I wondered if I should have been working at that farm instead.

Then the conversation moved to yacht races in Datça, and when I unfortunately said I had no knowledge of such topics due to my student budget, her husband joined in. Upon learning where I was studying, he asked questions in his Italian—which was better than mine—and after I answered in my own broken Italian, I took my revenge with my own game in French when I found out he was currently learning it. For some reason, the white wine bottles—the first choice in Mani—were emptying from the side. As the conversation progressed from Göbeklitepe to why the Ottomans were the heirs of the Trojans, my recollection of the talk began to fade; but what I remember clearly is that they were kind enough to invite me to their farm for riding.

I would have liked to continue further, but I’ve begun to feel that the stories might become monotonous, and I want to save my writing energy for a future post with a guest—a young individual whom I respect deeply, both in their field and in life in general, and whom I find exceptionally qualified. When this post is published, I will still be staying in Greece; as I write this, I haven't yet spent any time in Athens. I would have liked to include them as well, but for the sake of not letting this piece grow too long, I believe this is enough.

> "Simplicity, simplicity, simplicity! I say, let your affairs be as two or three, and not a hundred or a thousand; instead of a million count half a dozen, and keep your accounts on your thumb-nail."
