Hello. While every other student seems to have already started their summer break, finals at my dear school, Politecnico di Torino, keep going until mid-July. I have grown rather tired of classes by now, so while I am supposed to be studying, I keep looking for excuses to stop and drift into daydreaming instead. One of those daydreams took me back a few years, to the summer just before I moved to Italy.

That summer, before leaving Denizli, I wanted to go one last time to Pamukkale, Hierapolis, and Laodikeia. While reading the information panels there, I realized that one of the most important ancient cities of Western Anatolia had been excavated and brought back into view by the architectural circle of the very school I was about to attend. Just before going to Politecnico di Torino, encountering another trace of Turin behind an ancient city in Denizli felt like a very delicate and personal coincidence.

Later, while looking through my school's architectural archives, I found a few sources on Hierapolis and began reading them. The more I read, the more I found myself thinking this: perhaps my life has always been spent passing through places, but I have never perceived those places merely as backdrops. I have always related to them physically and emotionally, almost as if they were living things.

A small reader's note before I continue: in the first part of this essay, I spend a little while explaining why these stones feel so personal to me. This is the part that matters most to me, because I do not think of Hierapolis, Laodikeia, or any ancient city merely as historical information. I think of them as places through which a person builds a sense of self. But if you would rather go directly to the ancient cities, the urban planning, and the relationship between Hierapolis and Laodikeia, you can start from the section titled "The Two Faces of the Valley."

## I. A Personal Beginning: Making Kinship With Stones
One of the earliest examples of this relationship was the Camlik neighborhood in Denizli, where I grew up. Our house was close both to the military zone and to one of the largest parks in the city; so part of my childhood passed under red pines, staring up at long pine trees. There were also certain neighborhoods whose location I unfortunately cannot share, where I would collect and eat pine nuts with my grandmother, who carried within her a portion of Anatolia's countless reservoirs of practical knowledge. For many people, those pine nuts were a source of income. For me, they were part of how Denizli entered my body. My relationship with the city was not made only of streets and houses, but also of things like this.

But Pamukkale and the ancient cities around it were another side of that relationship. I think I first began to think about roots there.

When I walked through Hierapolis as a child, I would imagine that people had actually lived there. There were not only columns, theatres, and stones. Someone had walked from one place to another in the morning, carried water, bought something, died, and been buried there. What felt strange to me was not that the stones were old, but that this old life stood so close to present-day Denizli.

![Hierapolis](/images/photo1.png)

As a child, I often went to Hierapolis with my family. Because of his profession, my father would explain to me the engineering behind the structures, the intentions behind the urban planning, and the history of the city. But he did not do this dryly; he told it in a way that tickled my imagination, almost fantastically. So while walking among dead and worn stones, I imagined the people who had lived there. Someone had passed through these streets, sat in this theatre, stood by this water, walked from one end of this city to the other.

At the time, I tied this to a very simple thought: "Wow, my ancestors must have walked around and lived here centuries ago." For a child, there is probably no more natural thought than that. You assume the past of the place you live in is also your own past, because the world has not yet been divided into nations, alphabets, and the sharp categories of history textbooks.

One day in primary school, when I came here again with a friend, I tried to share the oddness of this feeling. He said something I had not expected at all: "These are other people's ancestors. Look, even their alphabet is not like ours." That sentence crushed me more than I would have expected at that age. It felt as if all the stones I had just believed to be mine had been taken out of my hands.

Fortunately, that evening I went home and asked my father whether this was true. He told me my friend was wrong, and for the first time introduced me to the idea of exponential growth. If you go back far enough, he explained, a person has far more ancestors than they imagine; in fact, by that point my grandfathers and grandmothers would probably have multiplied enough to fill the entire population of the city. I think that evening I both relaxed a little and felt, for the first time, that history was not only a distant thing sitting inside books.

![Hierapolis](/images/photo2.png)

Here I need to take the subject into a slightly thornier place; but I think the essay cannot really stand without putting on this burning shirt. In mainstream history education, when we are asked about our origins, we are often told only about the ancestors who became culturally dominant. A narrative is built as if the entire existence of people living in Turkey today consisted solely of nomadic communities from the steppes of Central Asia.

Please do not misunderstand me. Many of the things I love most about my identity really do come from Central Asian cultural inheritance. Nomadism, animal motifs, the idea of being in motion, a certain distance from the state, the pattern on the carpet, the horse in the story, certain textures in the language; all of these feel close to me. But I am not dressed only in these things. I am also shaped by the cultural accumulation left by the lands I have lived on: the Aegean, the Mediterranean, Phrygia, Caria, and the local life that still continues today.

Modern nation-state historiography often writes history backwards. It takes today's nations and projects them into the past as if they had always existed as sealed boxes that never changed. Yet in the past, people were not "nations" in today's sense. They were constantly shifting communities formed through language, religion, dynasty, city, tribe, trade route, marriage, war, and migration.

We can see this even when looking at Central Asian history. Turks, Mongols, Tungusic peoples, and other steppe communities were in contact for very long periods through kinship, wars, alliances, and cultural exchange. Even the things we call "Proto-Turkic" or "Proto-Mongolic" today are often not old nations with passports, but modern concepts trying to understand linguistic and cultural differentiation. I do not find it very convincing to imagine them as completely separate, pure, closed nations in today's sense just because one community moved toward southern Siberia and another toward the west.

Human beings are members of a much wider, much more tangled, and much older human family. I think the point is not to say, "We are actually this." It is to be able to say, "We are not only this."

I recently noticed how these regions seep into one another through something very simple while I was in Mani: food. During my stay, my employer often made wild greens sauteed or cooked with plenty of olive oil. I ate them without the slightest sense of strangeness, even with real appetite. Sitting down to those plates reminded me of the extremely scientific and diplomatic debates I used to have with a friend from the Black Sea region about Aegean and Black Sea famine foods.

While making fun of my friend from Ordu for the wild greens of his own region, I must admit that I show unnecessary fanaticism toward the olive-oil versions of the greens from my own region. Yes, this is a bit of regional campaigning. But perhaps people are naturally inclined to think their own famine food is nobler than someone else's. Still, to give credit where it is due: some regional dishes really are too horrifying to defend.

What surprised me in Mani, however, was this: even two people from the same country can differ so much through food, yet in another country, inside another language, I found myself eating a plate that felt completely familiar. In that moment, I felt that similarity was not an abstract historical matter. Olive trees, stone houses, summer drought, goat paths, wild greens, and the smell of the sea sometimes create a kinship older than passports.

The world a person inhabits even changes the words they use to describe themselves. One of my favorite examples of this comes from when I first began learning Italian. I asked my dear Italian teacher Beatrice how I could describe my dark brown complexion in Italian. She told me: "pelle olivastra" - olive-toned skin.

I loved that expression. Describing a person's skin color through the olive did not feel like merely naming a shade; it felt like an entire geography touching the human body. And the word was not foreign to me. My grandmother used to call my brother "my green olive" because of his green eyes, and me "my black olive" because of my almost black, very dark brown eyes.

A new word I learned in one language had come to stand beside a form of affection from my childhood. The expression I used to describe myself in Italy connected back to my grandmother's voice in Denizli. I think this is exactly what I mean: the world you inhabit determines not only what you eat, where you walk, or which stones you feel close to, but also the metaphors through which you build yourself.

All of this is why, when I return to Denizli in thought, I now look differently at the two ancient cities I used to confuse as a child: Hierapolis and Laodikeia. Perhaps that confusion was not so childish after all. These two cities can be read as two different faces of the same Lycus / Curuksu Valley.

## II. The Two Faces of the Valley: Hierapolis and Laodikeia
Hierapolis feels to me like one of those cities born from the human desire to approach the unknown. This was not merely a place with a beautiful view. With its hot springs, travertine terraces, the Plutonion cave from which poisonous gases rose out of the ground, and its entire uncanny nature, it was a threshold that drew people in. Healing and fear, water and death, steam and sacredness stood in the same place.

The gods of the city's pagan period seem to confirm this character. The fact that the Temple of Apollo and the Plutonion were part of the same sacred environment shows that Hierapolis was not simply a bright, measured, rational city of Apollo. It also carried a more uncanny sacredness, one close to the underworld, prophecy, and the idea of death. Apollo here does not appear only as the god of music and light, but as a chthonic and oracular figure in contact with underworld powers. The Plutonion, meanwhile, was perceived as a threshold opening onto the world of Hades, or Pluto in the Roman name.

![Temple Of Apollo](/images/apollo.png)

This is where the older Anatolian layer matters to me. Before the city became a Hellenistic and then Roman urban space, this landscape was already charged with a sacredness produced by the mountain, the cave, the hot water, the vapors, and the terrifying fertility of the earth itself. The Politecnico source cautiously suggests that the cult around the Plutonion may have preceded the city and may have been connected to an indigenous cult of the Great Mother, Cybele. I find that possibility extremely fitting. Cybele, after all, is not a tidy Olympian figure placed neatly above nature; she is the motherly and frightening force of the Anatolian landscape, the mountain, the wild, the cave, the animal, the fertility that gives life and the earth that takes it back. So when Apollo later appears beside the Plutonion, I do not imagine a clean replacement of one god by another. I imagine something more layered: a Greek and Roman religious language settling over an older Anatolian awe toward the earth. Hierapolis, in that sense, was not only Apollo's city or Pluto's threshold; it may also have been carrying the memory of Cybele's older, more bodily, more terrestrial sacredness.

But Hierapolis did not remain a city abandoned to the strangeness of nature. After the earthquake of 60 CE, when it was rebuilt in the Roman period, a much more rational urban mind was drawn over this uncanny geography: orthogonal streets, rectangular blocks, a main avenue about 1.5 kilometers long, monumental gates, an agora, baths, a theatre, water and sewer systems. It was as if human beings had answered nature's irrational call with engineering. The mouth of the Plutonion opening toward the underworld and the measured Roman street stood side by side in the same city.

![Hierapolis](/images/oldhierapolis.png)

This is also where one can see, almost physically, how much of Europe's later language of rational order owes to the Roman inheritance. I do not mean that modern European rationalism simply begins in Rome, as if history were that clean. But the Roman instinct to measure, align, classify, drain, pave, connect, legislate, and make space administrable is one of the old ancestors of that habit. Even in a city like Hierapolis, founded around hot water, vapors, sacred caves, and a very irrational kind of natural attraction, Rome appears as a line drawn through uncertainty. The grid, the sewer, the aqueduct, the monumental gate, the public bath, the straight axis: all of them turn the frightening landscape into something that can be entered, crossed, used, taxed, restored, and understood.

One of the best ways to describe this rational face of Hierapolis is through the Doric architectural language. The Doric order places measure, repetition, and weight against all that hot water, steam, underworld fear, and the uncanniness of the Plutonion. It is not rational because it is emotionless; it is rational because it makes weight, rhythm, and proportion visible. In the Politecnico source, the portico surrounding the sacred area of Apollo is described as Doric; the latrine structure includes monolithic Doric columns; and around the Frontinus road there is a Doric colonnaded urban axis. In other words, the city does not simply surrender to nature's strangeness. It makes it legible through columns, porticoes, roads, and proportions.

But Hierapolis is not made only of Doric simplicity. In other parts of the city, more graceful and more ornate architectural languages appear. For instance, in the residential structure known as the "House of the Ionic Capitals," Ionic capitals are used around the peristyle; here the architecture shifts into a more domestic, representative, and delicate tone. In places such as the theatre stage and the nymphaeum, Corinthian or Composite capitals bring forward a more ostentatious Roman language. Water, stage, sculpture, and ornament become part of public display.

Seen this way, the architecture of Hierapolis can be read not as the language of one single style, but as the language of different human needs. The Doric order gives the city measure and public weight; the Ionic order gives a more refined tone to domestic and representative spaces; Corinthian and Composite orders carry the more decorative, theatrical side of the Roman period.

One of the things that made Hierapolis so uncanny and attractive was its natural healing sources. Hot water, travertine, steam, and the poisonous mouth of the Plutonion opening toward the underworld turned the city into not merely a place where people lived, but a threshold where the limits of the body could be felt. A person who came here did not simply bathe; they thought about healing, aging, and death within the same landscape.

The great necropoleis surrounding Hierapolis strengthen this feeling. As a place visited by those seeking healing, by pilgrims, by travelers, and perhaps by wealthy people who wished to approach the healing power of hot water in the later stages of life, the city seems to shorten the distance between life and death. The body healed by hot water and the body preparing for the grave passed through the same roads, the same gates, the same white stones. This is why Hierapolis appears to me not only as a city of healing, but as a city where death and life come too close to one another.

In the Christian period, Hierapolis continued this sacred character in another form. According to tradition, Saint Philip, one of the apostles of Jesus, was martyred here. In Late Antiquity, on the hill to the east of the city, a large octagonal Martyrium - a martyr's shrine - was built in his memory. Thus the older sacredness around the Plutonion and Apollo did not simply disappear; it transformed into another layer by giving way to Christianity's memory of martyrdom and pilgrimage.

![Grave Of San Filippo](/images/sanfilippo.png)

At the other end of the valley, however, a different kind of life was unfolding in Laodikeia. If Hierapolis looked toward the fragility of the body, healing, and death, Laodikeia was more the city of production, trade, public life, and wealth. There, the urban plan makes the everyday sides of life visible: main streets, agoras, a stadium, theatres, baths, a water system, shops, textile and dye production. In Hierapolis, one thinks around water, sacredness, and death; in Laodikeia, one lives more inside commerce, fabric, spectacle, sport, politics, and the everyday crowd.

In the New Testament, in Revelation 3:15-17, the church in Laodikeia is addressed as follows:

> ""I know your works: you are neither cold nor hot. I wish that you were either cold or hot. So, because you are lukewarm, and neither hot nor cold, I will spit you out of my mouth. For you say, 'I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing,' not realizing that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked.""

It is a very strong interpretation to think that these words in the New Testament rest on Laodikeia's water geography. On one side stood the hot waters of Hierapolis; on the other, the cold springs of Colossae; in the middle stood Laodikeia, forced to bring its water from distant sources. Laodikeia's water was neither the hot healing water of Hierapolis nor the cold, refreshing water of Colossae. By the time it reached the city, it became lukewarm, stripped of its function, almost tasteless as an image.

But the harshness of the text seems directed not only at water, but also at the self-satisfied state created by the city's wealth. Laodikeia may have been a rich city, but in the critique of the New Testament this wealth seems to have produced not vitality, but complacency. How much this prosperity affected Laodikeia's spiritual posture is something I would rather leave to the reader.

Laodikeia, too, reveals something of its soul through its god. If the divine world of Hierapolis is related to the underworld, hot water, prophecy, and death, then in the older memory of Laodikeia stands a more celestial and powerful figure: Zeus. One of the city's earlier names was Diospolis, meaning "the city of Zeus." Even that name suits the character of Laodikeia. Because Laodikeia was a city turned toward the more ambitious, productive, public side of life: trade, textile, money, roads, agoras, stadiums, baths, and wealth.

Later, the city was named Laodikeia after Laodike, the wife of the Seleucid king Antiochus II. But the name Diospolis seems to reveal a deeper city spirit: while Hierapolis looked toward the underworld, steam, and death, Laodikeia looked toward the sky, power, and the order of production.
