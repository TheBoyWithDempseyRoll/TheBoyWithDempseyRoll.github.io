This Thing of Ours, The Borderline Era: The Sopranos

May 20, 2026 · 20 min · Alp Yağcıoğlu

Tragedy is classically defined as a dramatic narrative in which a protagonist—often a figure of power—is brought to ruin or profound sorrow. This descent typically unfolds in three fundamental stages: first, the Hamartia (the fatal character flaw or poisoned roots that sow the seeds of destruction); second, the Peripeteia (the turning point or reversal, where the illusion of free will is tested and terrible choices are made); and finally, the Catastrophe (the ultimate ruin, the absolute loss of humanity, and the surrender to the void).

Tony Soprano: I don’t know. The morning of the day I got sick, I’d been thinking. It’s good to be in something from the ground floor. I came too late for that, I know. But lately, I’m getting the feeling that I came in at the end. The best is over.

Dr. Melfi: Many Americans, I think, feel that way.

Tony Soprano: I think about my father. He never reached the heights like me. But in a lot of ways, he had it better. He had his people, they had their standards, they had pride. Today, what do we got?

Here, Tony is pointing to something much deeper than just being at the tail end of the mafia business. In fact, he is mourning the modern era—his father’s era—while highlighting the ultimate motto of his own age: “anything goes.”

Before diving deeper, it is crucial to emphasize that in discussing the concepts of the Mafia and La Cosa Nostra, I am describing the ideal construct in Tony’s mind—precisely how I believe he understood them. For Tony, the organized crime family was his reality and his life’s purpose; indeed, in later episodes of the series, he explicitly states that he is at war and believes he has no religious obligations or moral debts. In his eyes, La Cosa Nostra was something ideal and existential, inherited from “the old country.” Therefore, I cannot feel at ease without repeatedly stating that I have absolutely no intention of romanticizing organized crime—especially as someone born and raised in Turkey, and currently living in Italy, both being countries where the Mafia remains a severe, deeply rooted problem that has turned countless lives into hell. Attentive readers will already understand that I am discussing what La Cosa Nostra meant to Tony, rather than what it is in reality.

Act I: Hamartia (The Tragic Flaw)#

In the era of Tony’s father, there was omertà—the silence about La Cosa Nostra, “this thing of ours.” But its etymological roots run much deeper, revealing a profound ontological stance. Linguistic research suggests that the word was actually born from the gradual fusion of two distinct concepts over time: umiltà—meaning humility or submission—and the Sicilian word omu (meaning manhood or humanity, sharing the same root as the Latin homo and the Spanish hombre). It was a code that demanded the ultimate ‘manliness’ to remain humble, resilient, and utterly silent in the face of authority.

This phenomenon was so potent that, in the past, the people of Sicily could communicate complex ideas using only subtle gestures and glances, without even needing to say ‘Cosa’—the thing. While this example arises from a negative context, it perfectly illustrates how individuals were able to ground themselves in something absolute and far greater than their own selves.

To truly understand why this code emerged, we must look at the tragic history of Sicily. Ontologically, the origin of this concept is tied to an island that was constantly subjugated by shifting foreign occupiers throughout history—Greeks, Romans, Arabs, Normans, the French, and the Spanish. For centuries, the official ‘State’ in Sicily was never a protector of the people; it was an alien oppressor, a distant entity that only existed to extract wealth and impose harsh laws. The law did not belong to the Sicilians, so they fundamentally rejected it.

Consequently, in the absence of a trustworthy state, the people had to create their own parallel system of justice, protection, and survival. They managed their society from within, in a completely insular and clandestine way. This alternative, underground state became known as Cosa Nostra—literally, ’this thing of ours.’ It was a brotherhood built on the premise that the only people you could rely on were your own. In Tony’s father’s time, ’the thing of ours’ hadn’t yet been completely cheapened by late-stage capitalist greed or become fodder for common gossip; family members were still faithful to the oath they had taken, carrying the weight of that ancient Sicilian survival mechanism. Yet today, in every imaginable field, there exist only tiny, mortal, atomized particles of chaos.

I don’t believe Tony is the only one mourning the past. Dr. Melfi notes that most Americans feel this way, and in my view, the overwhelming majority of the world has already become a “mini-America.” By its very foundation, America was a country built by those driven by sheer desperation and the willingness to risk everything. Aside from Columbus, who first set foot there, the nation was shaped by those fleeing famine in Ireland to face terrifying, death-defying voyages across a storm-battered Atlantic, British convicts, Scots, or those venturing from Southern Italy to completely unknown, alien lands, just for the slimmest chance at a new life. Years later, this taught us that to succeed in life, you had to take risks. That is precisely why every American learns poker almost before they even learn to speak. However, we no longer have an expanding world; instead, we are all playing a game of musical chairs, occupying each other’s tiny spaces, and we have become more obsessed with our boundaries than ever before. Because I know all too well—even at the supermarket checkout—with what meticulous care you use that grocery divider to separate products you haven’t even officially claimed as your own yet. They call that tool Abstandshalter in German, which literally means ‘distance keeper’; isn’t that beautiful?

To understand how this sickness manifests, we must first look at the man himself. If we are to describe Tony Soprano, he is a masculine, tall, and solidly built man in his mid-30s. He is highly adept socially, possessing what could be called “alpha male” characteristics, and is remarkably successful and respected in his line of work. In his adult life, he is not very practiced at dealing with not getting what he wants, simply because he can be with any woman he desires, and his wealth can afford him anything he could possibly wish for.

So how is it that this ‘ideal’ man finds himself in a psychiatrist’s chair, stricken by panic attacks? Tony is enamored with the wild Canadian ducks that land in his pool; he nurtures them, yet the moment he witnesses them fly away, he collapses. Tony is so terrified of the uncertainty of the future and the transience of all things that his body manifests a panic attack, which is simply a slight tic of the ‘fight or flight’ response.

And yet, a profound sense of transience permeates every aspect of his life. The glorious golden age of his father’s era has come to an end, and now, everyone is merely looking out for their own interests. We know all too well that Tony lives in perpetual fear—whether it is the constant paranoia of an informant turning state’s evidence due to the RICO Act, or the relentless dread of those around him plotting his assassination.

(At this point, a brief caveat is necessary: very recently, while riding the bus, I witnessed everyone mobilize to help a disabled person get on board. This is something we are accustomed to, a staple of our shared morality. Yet today, it prompted me to reflect: why do we help? In a strictly utilitarian sense, they might not offer us any direct physical return; moreover, on a broader scale, our taxes are spent to build ramps, lifts, and dedicated spaces on public transport for them. To be clear, I am not remotely bothered by this, but from a purely superficial standpoint, it is not “rational.” The real reason we do it is to maintain a collective societal pact—to know that if any of us were to find ourselves in that position one day, we too would be protected. For a social animal like the human being, this is a vital spiritual reassurance.

For Tony, however, not even the continuity of his life or freedom is guaranteed. As a side note, though, we must not forget that while disability rights may be protected on paper today, the extent to which the healthcare rights of the economically disadvantaged are actually safeguarded remains highly debatable.)

His daughter—the greatest symbol of innocence in his life—has begun to set sail toward adolescence and adulthood, while the influential elders in his life have been slowly burning away within that hell we call time. And psychologically, Tony has simply been unable to cope with it all.

While we will delve deeper into this later using Freudian psychoanalytic techniques, for now, we must address Tony’s fixation on Gary Cooper—that ‘strong and silent’ type. This idealization stems from Tony’s internal blueprint of the ideal man: a relic of a golden age who fights tempests within himself without ever letting a tremor show on the surface. Because, as you sense, everyone ultimately dies in their own arms. Who can you trust enough to entrust your existence to? Are you not striving, in your own way, to reach your most perfect and unattainable self?

Meanwhile, as Tony struggles with these panic attacks and takes Prozac, he hides it from all his associates to avoid appearing weak, while simultaneously berating himself for failing to be a Gary Cooper.

As the series progresses, both through life itself and through his sessions with Dr. Melfi, we—along with Tony—get the chance to see who he really is and what his true goals are. Dr. Melfi introduces him to his parents all over again. It is best to start with his mother, Livia Soprano.

Livia herself is likely someone cursed with responsibilities she never wanted to have, taking out the pain of not having lived as she wished on the world; she is defined by Dr. Melfi as having Borderline Personality Disorder. She is an extremely rude and manipulative woman. Referring to the DSM-5, the following can be cited as examples of her borderline traits:

  • Transient, stress-related paranoid ideation: She is highly suspicious of others’ motives, constantly believing that people are plotting against her. When she learns that her son is seeing a psychiatrist, her first reaction, instead of worrying about her son’s health, is to worry about what he is telling his psychiatrist about her. This intense paranoia is so severe that it famously leads her to subtly green-light a hit on her own son, Tony.
  • Frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment: Livia constantly uses guilt trips and plays the ultimate victim (often reminding everyone she gave her life to her children “on a silver platter”) to manipulate her family into staying close, ensuring she is never left alone.

Livia Soprano: I wasn’t always perfect, but I always tried to do the best I could. And I know you didn’t, any of you, didn’t like it when I tried to tell you what to do. Babies are like animals, they are no different from dogs. Somebody has to teach them right and wrong. But I was your mother. Who else was gonna do it? If you ask me, I did a pretty damn good job. The palace you live in, up there on that hill… I gave my children their lives on a silver platter. I suppose you’re not going to kiss me?

Tony: What?

Livia Soprano: You’re cruel, that’s what you are.

As Tony abruptly left the house and hurried away, he tripped and fell; Livia’s only reaction to this was simply to laugh.

  • Chronic feelings of emptiness: This is the core of her existence. Livia embodies the “it’s all a big nothing” philosophy. Nothing brings her joy, she is completely devoid of inner peace, and she actively drains the happiness from everyone around her like a black hole.

AJ: What’s the purpose of being?

Livia: Why does everything have to have a purpose? The world is a jungle. And if you want my advice, Anthony, don’t expect happiness. You won’t get it. People let you down. In the end, you die in your own arms.

AJ: You mean, alone?

Livia: It’s all a big nothing. What makes you think you’re so special?

  • Inappropriate, intense anger or difficulty controlling anger: Livia is notoriously vindictive. While her anger isn’t always physically explosive, it manifests as a deeply toxic, passive-aggressive hostility directed at anyone who dares to cross her or challenge her narrative.

Because of having such a mother, Tony was never able to experience unconditional maternal love since childhood; consequently, constantly drawn into the black hole of nihilism, he carries a deep-seated melancholy that is both hereditary and imprinted upon him by his upbringing. After his mother’s death, while watching television late at night, Tony could not hold back his tears upon seeing a mother on a program treating her son with kindness. Throughout his entire life, he sought the affection he could never receive from her in the arms of other women.

As for his father, Johnny Boy Soprano, he was a successful, well-liked, and respected mobster, just like Tony. Tony was accustomed to his absence, but he was not hostile toward him; rather, he admired him. His father’s constant affairs with goomahs and the way he brought home the bacon through violence led Tony to believe that this was the natural order of things. When he witnessed his father cutting off a butcher’s finger as punishment for an unpaid debt and bringing home free meat from that same butcher—and then saw his mother derive a sensual pleasure from this display—it pushed him to conclude that resorting to violence was a necessary method for survival and providing for the family.

Furthermore, he learned that his father suffered from fainting spells due to panic attacks; he had even heard the story of his grandfather fainting on a donkey cart back in Avellino in the old country. Seeing these same symptoms manifest firsthand in his own son, A.J., finally confirmed for Tony that he had a hereditary predisposition for panic attacks.

Act II: Peripeteia (The Turning Point)#

As Tony begins his psychiatric sessions and is reintroduced to his parents through Dr. Melfi’s psychotherapy, he initially feels a sense of shame regarding his parents’ efforts and blames himself for his own shortcomings. However, Dr. Melfi’s sole objective is for Tony to gain awareness of the behavioral patterns that shape his identity and to help him strive to break free from this schema.

Tony, however, does not view it that way. In his eyes, Dr. Melfi has led him to believe that the bad things he does are not intrinsic to his nature but rather something he was condemned to, effectively turning the sessions into a place of confession and validation. Even the intellectual and professional boundaries within Dr. Melfi had to struggle against Tony Soprano’s charisma until the very end of the series.

Ever since psychotherapy integrated into our lives, I have observed a profound misalignment between its true objective and what people actually seek from it in practice. The genuine purpose of psychotherapy is to cultivate meta-awareness through deep reflection, enabling us to focus on our actions and exert conscious willpower over our character and behavior. However—and this is a vast subject in itself—everything seemingly new is merely a reassembly of very old fragments. Consider this: for most people, what truly separates going to a psychotherapist from entering a confessional booth? Rooted in our collective unconscious memory, contemporary psychotherapy, as far as I can see, often strays far from actual healing, transforming instead into a mere mechanism for self-validation. Coupled with the modern illusion that the entire universe revolves around our internal state, our very approach to this treatment is ontologically flawed. Ultimately, I must leave my own question mark here as to whether psychotherapy has genuinely been a successful remedy for humanity, and move forward before provoking the wrath of the experts in the field.

As Tony’s life progresses, he constantly witnesses the hypocrisy of the people around him. His wife, Carmela, continuously acts like a devout Catholic, yet turns a blind eye to all his crimes the moment she is placated with jewelry and luxury goods. He describes his closest friends to his psychiatrist as jackals; his best friend, Pussy Bonpensiero, betrayed him to avoid prison; and his nephew, Christopher, is an incorrigible drug addict. The internal contradictions of all these people provide Tony with an excuse—one that I believe most of us also share—to legitimize his own flaws by pointing to the mistakes of others.

But on the other hand, consider how he handles Jackie Jr., the son of his late closest friend who was explicitly entrusted to his care. Tony effectively has him killed merely because the boy became a snag in his business operations. In truth, his disgust for the boy had taken root long before that final transgression; he is repulsed by Jackie Jr. precisely because the young man is a mirror reflecting Tony’s own darkest traits—namely, his propensity for violence and his pathetic habit of masking infidelity with luxury gifts. Yet, Tony is so terrified of his own free will, and so desperate to evade the moral weight of his actions, that he masterfully manipulates his own friend into giving the execution order instead.

When he falls into a coma, in a deeply existential sequence, he refuses to hand over the briefcase that represents his life. He rejects becoming Kevin Finnerty, ultimately claiming his identity as Tony Soprano, for better or worse. And from this moment on, he has finally made his free choice.

Upon waking from the coma, he reads a quote on his hospital wall, allegedly rooted in ancient Ojibwe wisdom: “Sometimes I go about in pity for myself, and all the while, a great wind carries me across the sky.” The realization of being merely a small cog within a massive machine deeply unsettles him on the inside. Yet, he tries to project the image of a man who has received this quote with dignity and learned a profound lesson from it.

Act III: Catastrophe (The Ruin)#

However, as we approach the end of the series, we witness just how deeply selfish he has become and how completely he has detached himself from empathy. When his nephew Christopher hands over the love of his life, Adriana—effectively sending her to her death for Tony’s sake—we as the audience expect Tony to have a heartfelt conversation with Chris about his agonizing pain. Instead, all Tony cares to ask is how much Adriana actually knew and how much trouble she is going to cause him. In the times that follow, he even goes so far as to characterize Chris’s inability to kill Adriana with his own hands as a pathetic sign of weakness.

This culminates in the aftermath of their severe car accident. When Christopher could have easily been saved, Tony suffocates him, coldly rationalizing the murder in his own mind. He feels a terrifying, overwhelming sense of relief at Chris’s death, without experiencing a single second of genuine sorrow. In fact, he is actively disgusted by everyone around him who genuinely mourns the loss.

And later, when he travels to Vegas, sleeps with a woman Chris used to be involved with, and embarks on a psychedelic peyote trip with her in the desert, he joyfully shouts at the sunrise: “I get it!” At that moment, he no longer believes that the ‘great wind’ is carrying him; rather, he feels that he dictates the wind, and that everything is moving exactly as he wills it. It is here, ultimately, that Tony surrenders the very last shred of his humanity to the void.

At the very end of the series, when he visits his uncle—who has lost most of his memory to old age—and sees that the old man no longer has any recollection whatsoever of ’this thing of ours,’ which once meant absolutely everything to him, the nihilism inside Tony is finally and utterly validated. Whatever happens to him from this point onward will no longer make any difference.

I find it incredibly useful to think of humanity and society not as an atomized mass, but as a single, collective human being stretching across time—and Homo sapiens is a beautifully chosen name for this individual.

The name was coined in 1758 by Carl Linnaeus, derived from two distinct Latin roots: Homo, coming from humus, meaning soil or earth—signifying the mortal, flesh-bound creature of the ground—and Sapiens, from the verb sapere, meaning to taste, to know, and crucially, to discern. Thus, this collective individual is, by definition, the Discerning Mortal.

From the moment this Discerning Mortal stepped onto the stage of history, its singular, paramount purpose was simply to sustain its existence for as long as possible; understanding why it existed was always a secondary priority. To safeguard this continuity, humanity designed a series of rules, myths, and social pacts to live by.

Yet today, we no longer know if modern man’s objective is actually to live. The very concept of “living” has been so thoroughly muddied and overcomplicated that the collective organism is now being consumed from within by its own cancerous cells. For the vast majority, true living is no longer the target; instead, each atomized cell aggressively looks out only for its minor fraction of space, completely blind to the fact that its selfishness accelerates the death of the whole.

As for the ancient series of rules that once held the organism together, they have been completely dismantled by postmodernism. We abandoned the grand narratives, celebrating them as mere chains wrapped around human reason and freedom. But in doing so, we committed a fatal error: we discarded the grand purpose behind them. That purpose was the ultimate anchor of our existence—a drive deeply intertwined with a primordial vitality far older than language itself.

According to Yalçın Küçük, what separates man from Homo sapiens is called the ‘humanization’ process. Being human is not a biological trait; it is being a creature that can intervene in history, has ideals, and can rebel for the sake of those ideals. Chivalric romances and the art of the novel are the written records of this adventure of becoming human. Transcending mere survival, eating, and reproducing is the key to being human.

According to Hegel, on the other hand, to be human is to be capable of fighting for a purely abstract concept. That is why Don Quixote attacks the windmills; he rejects the safe and ordinary life of the animal called Homo sapiens and endures suffering for the sake of the human virtues in his mind.

Before humanity could fully evolve into “humanhood,” it moved forward guided solely by the primal purpose assigned to it. Yet, unlike every other living being sharing that very same goal, it acquired a unique faculty: the capability for timeless, eternal thought. A lion cub on the savannah cannot fathom reducing its hunting today out of fear that prey might become scarce in the future; yet, a human being can. Ultimately, because the true purpose of this faculty is to push existence as far as it can possibly go, anything a human does outside of this grand design will inevitably crush them under the weight of an existential, destructive misalignment.

I don’t know if the tragedy of modern man and the man on the edge is any different from Tony Soprano’s tragedy. Now, looking at this text from start to finish, aren’t most of us at some stage of a tragedy?

It is my personal belief that if humanity were to lean its back against a phenomenon far greater than its own limited life and biology—anchoring itself to a transcendent ’thing of ours’ belonging to all humans, true to our collective nature rooted in reason or biology—it could very well be the Garden of Eden. Even if it is never meant to be, I want to dream of a world inhabited by people who are capable of seeing beyond their own skin.

Personally, I wish for every human being to strive to look beyond the horizon.

As Ferhan Şensoy said: Utopias are beautiful.
